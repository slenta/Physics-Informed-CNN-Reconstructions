	\chapter{Results} \label{results}
	
	\section{Present: Argo-Era Reconstructions} \label{analysis_argo}
	
	At first, the U-net is trained with assimilations as well as masks only from the Argo-Era. Thus, it is set up as well as possible to reproduce Argo-Era assimilations. The training process is then conducted until the loss parameters do not show any significant decrease anymore. Manual selection has shown that the most important loss parameters for the reconstruction of masked temperature profiles are those monitoring the direct misfit of each gridpoint and the total variation loss between neighboring gridpoints. Thus, their multiplicators have been upgraded in this version of the convolutional U-net and their development will be shown to indicate the state of training of the neural network. \\
	
	\begin{figure}[H]
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=1.0\linewidth]{plots/Loss/Training_loss_overall_smoothing_1}
		\caption{Mean Hole and Valid training loss for all depth layers over 600.000 training iterations of the neural network.}
		\label{fig:loss_argo}
	\end{figure}
	
	
	After 600.000 iterations, both the hole and valid loss depicted in figure \ref{fig:loss_argo} have reached a plateau with the hole loss showing a higher variability. The loss curves for all individual depth layers are attached in section \ref{anhang}. An analysis of both loss functions shows that the network state with the lowest combined loss occurs at approximately 585.000 iterations. The state of the neural network at that loss minimum is therefore utilized for further analysis. \\

	Within the Argo-Era, the neural network is able to reconstruct assimilations masked with observational patterns within the assimilation's uncertainty range for monthly values (figure \ref{fig:argo_timeseries_assi}). Not only do both uncertainty ranges overlap, with a correlation of 0.99 the fit is clearly better than just inside of one standard deviation. The neural network's reconstruction even manages to capture monthly trends in the assimilation very well. Analyzing this timeseries further, the misfit between both estimates depicted in figure \ref{fig:misfit_argo_assi} shows that between 2009 and 2014 the reconstruction possesses a slight cool bias compared to the assimilation. But, as the remaining timeseries does not show any clear bias but rather just errors distributed around zero, it can be assumed to be insignificant and not a sign of a larger uncertainty within the network reconstructions. Figure \ref{fig:misfit_argo_assi} also shows that even during the Argo-Era, the uncertainty of the OHC estimates significantly declines in the first few years before the Argo floats reached global coverage. \\	
	Figure \ref{fig:assi_201801} further underlines the great agreement between both OHC estimates. It shows the NA OHC as calculated by the assimilation and neural network in January 2008. The corresponding input masked assimilation is shown in section \ref{anhang}. Not only does the network's overall OHC agree with the EnKF assimilation, but also all major spatial OHC patterns are represented well by the neural network. Even on this monthly scale, the neural network is able to capture small scale features, for example at the North African coast, in the North-Western NA or even most of the eddy like structures of the North Atlantic current in the south of the SPG. \\

	To further analyse the neural network's capability to capture the assimilation's spatial patterns, the pattern correlation between both OHC estimates can be taken. The pattern correlation is calculated by flattening both OHC images to a one-dimensional array and taking the anomaly correlation coefficient of both arrays. Thus, the similarities between spatial patterns for both OHC images can be estimated. Figure \ref{fig:patterncorr_assi_argo} depicts this pattern correlation during the Argo-Era. During the first years of the Argo-Era both OHC estimates show a slightly lower pattern correlation while with rising observations during the Argo-Era the pattern correlation reaches a constant value of over 0.8 with an overall mean pattern correlation of 0.86. The first 5 years also show higher month-to-month variability in pattern correlation. This can also be attributed to the higher inconsistencies in data coverage during the first years of the Argo project. \\

	\section{Past: Extension to pre-Argo-Era} \label{analysis_preargo}
	
	
	In a next analysis step, the time window of reconstruction is extended to the pre-Argo-Era. In order to prepare the neural network for the reconstruction of the observational patterns of the pre-Argo-Era, the training process has to include pre-Argo masks. Therefore, the training data, which still only stems from the Argo-Era, is masked with observational patterns from the entire period from 1958--2020. Otherwise, the neural network's parameters are kept constant. \\		
	Even after 1.000.000 training iterations, the network's loss functions over all depth layers are still decreasing significantly (figure \ref{fig:loss_full_1}). The loss developments of all individual depth layers show very similar behavior and can be further examined in chapter \ref{anhang}. Usually, these training curves would indicate that the training should be resumed until a stable plateau is reached. However, the overall validation error, which depicts the mean mismatch between the OHC timeseries of the held back evaluation assimilation member and the neural network reconstruction, suggests that no further improvement in the reconstruction accuracy occurs (figure \ref{fig:loss_full_2}). Instead, utilizing a network state around iteration 550.000 might yield the best reconstruction results. This stopping point also agrees with the loss minimum in the hole loss function. Thus, this network state after 550.000 iterations is utilized for the following analysis.\\

		\caption{Hole and valid training loss for the first 1.000.000 training iterations of the neural network.}

		\label{fig:loss_full_2}

	The results of the following reconstruction, which can be viewed in figure \ref{fig:timeseriesfullassiannual}, show higher disagreements between both OHC estimates in the pre-Argo-Era compared to the previous Argo-Era reconstruction. Nonetheless, with an anomaly correlation of 0.93, they still agree very well with each other. In comparison to the first part of the analysis, this plot shows the annual running means of both OHC timeseries. During the pre-Argo-Era, the monthly fluctuations in observational coverage lead to higher variability in both assimilation and neural network reconstruction. Taking the running mean of both timeseries clearifies the analysis, because outlying months, which are results of very low amounts of observations in unusual regions, distort the image less. \\	
	Supported by the depiction of the misfit between both timeseries in figure \ref{fig:misfit_full_assi}, it becomes obvious that the difference between both timeseries consists of more than just increased variability due to higher uncertainty caused by less observations. Instead, some trends become obvious in this annual timeseries. Firstly, the assimilation shows much higher interannual variability and a clear peak in OHC that the reconstruction does not capture during the first ten years of the timeseries. Secondly, the neural network shows a consistent warm bias during the following 20 years. The two main options as causes for these differences are on the one hand an overfitting of the neural network to the training period. The possibility that the neural network does not recognize assimilation patterns from the beginning of the pre-Argo-Era, if they do not resemble those of the training period, has to be considered. On the other hand, it is also possible that the differences are caused just by the shift in observational density between the Argo and pre-Argo-Era. Then, these differences could be utilized as indicators of uncertainties in the assimilation beyond its ensemble spread. \\

		\label{fig:misfit_full_assi}

	Figure \ref{fig:timeseries_assi_full_maskargo} supports this second hypothesis. It shows reconstructions over the same period, with the difference that the observational patterns of the Argo-Era are also used during the pre-Argo-Era to mask the assimilations. Therefore, this reconstruction gives an estimate on how much of the reconstruction uncertainty comes from the amount of observations. However, it also prevents the neural network from implementing assimilation patterns from the Argo-Era in the reconstruction of the pre-Argo assimilations. By using the Argo-Era observational patterns, the high observational density pushes the neural network very close to the assimilation without leaving it its own room for interpretation. One of the main assumption of this thesis is that the assimilation is more accurate over the training period. Therefore, the difference between reconstruction and assimilation cannot be interpreted as a fault of the neural network. Instead, it rather shows the uncertainty the assimilation incorporates, if the underlying model cannot be supported and nudged by sufficient observations. \\
	Closer examination of the reconstructions and assimilation during the first decade of the timeseries can help explain the observed uncertainty in that time period. In figure \ref{fig:timeseriesfullassiannual} the assimilation shows much stronger interannual variations in SPG OHC at the start of the 1960s than the neural network reconstructions. Koul (pers. comm.) has already examined similar EnKF assimilations during that period and found that the assimilation seems to possess much higher uncertainty during that time than its ensemble spread suggests. The biggest reason for this uncertainty is the dependence of the assimilation on observational input to correct for biases in the model, for example due to its positioning of the North Atlantic current. But also other uncertainties in the model get highlighted when there are no observations available to assist the assimilation in its estimate. The differences between both timeseries support that assumption. They show that the assimilation's behavior during this period is not consistent to the spatial patterns and geophysical processes during the neural network's training period. This assumption is underlined by the pattern correlation corresponding to figure \ref{fig:timeseriesfullassiannual}, which is depicted in figure \ref{fig:patcorassifullannual}. It shows a much lower pattern correlation during the pre-Argo-Era with a mean of 0.62 before starting to increase at the start of the new century to over 0.8 in the Argo-Era resulting in a mean pattern correlation over both eras of 0.70.

	Figure \ref{fig:ohcfullassi1960s} visualizes the OHC of both the assimilation and reconstruction during that time period in the form of a 10 year mean. Both versions differ most notably in the central NA, where the assimilation shows strong negative anomalies, while the neural network reconstruction depicts close to no anomalies in that region. Similar differences have already been described in other publications and can be related to what is known as the North Atlantic Current's \textit{North-West corner}. Many climate models, such as the MPI-ESM underlying this EnKF assimilation, are known to represent the gulf stream in the central NA further south than its observed flow. Therefore, the assimilation shows strong negative anomalies in the region of this North-West corner whenever there are no observations available to nudge the underlying model towards a correct representation of the gulf stream's flow. As the neural network is only trained in a period with high observational density in the entire NA, it only learns a correct representation of the gulf streams flow. Correspondingly, the reconstructions do not show the same negative anomalies in this region, when there are no observations available to suggest anything different.\\

	\end{figure}	
	
	Thus, it can also be assumed that parts of the differences between assimilation and neural network reconstruction during the first years can be attributed to model uncertainties similar to the false representation of the North-West corner. From the 1970s onward at least sporadic observations are available in the entire North Atlantic causing assimilation outputs more consistent with the neural network reconstruction. Figure \ref{fig:correlation} supports this analysis, as it shows that the gridwise correlation between reconstruction and assimilation is especially low in the region of the North-West corner during the first 10 years, while it depicts higher correlation in the rest of the pre-Argo-Era with the steady increase in the number of observations. The mismatch between both estimates suggests that during a time with such a low amount of observations the assimilation can be subject to model uncertainties and misrepresentations also outside of the North-West corner region. This conclusion would then mean that a neural network trained with assimilation data during a time with high observational density can be able to detect model uncertainties in specific regions. Additionally, it can provide an estimate of the overall uncertainty not contained within the assimilation's ensemble spread. \\

		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=1.0\linewidth]{plots/images/correlation_preargo_full}
		\caption{Grid-wise anomaly correlation between the EnKF assimilation and the neural network reconstruction of NA OHC in the years 1958 -- 1968 (left) and 1968 -- 2004 (right).}

	During the remainder of the 20th century reconstruction and assimilation mostly agree within their respective uncertainty ranges. Nonetheless, the reconstruction shows a positive bias until the beginning of the 1990s. This positive bias could still be a remainder of similar model uncertainties related to the observational density. Afterwards, the differences vanish slowly but steadily until the beginning of the Argo-Era. From thereon out, both timeseries are again in good agreement within one standard deviation of their ensemble spreads. \\

	
	\section{Direct Observations Reconstruction} \label{analysis_direct}
	
	The reconstruction of masked assimilation aims mainly at assessing the neural network's ability to reproduce EnKF assimilations and identifying uncertainties in the assimilation stemming from the underlying model. Another uncertainty that cannot be examined with this approach is the assimilation's integration of observations. Additionally, it is not possible to estimate the neural network's potential to replace data assimilation, when the neural network is never given actual observations as input. Thus, the neural network is now given the EN4 observations from 1958 until 2021 as input in a next evaluation step. To clarify the terminology, the reconstruction of observations by the neural network will from now on be called \textit{direct reconstructions}, while the reconstruction of masked assimilations will be referred to as \textit{indirect reconstructions}. As the direct reconstructions cannot reconstruct the assimilation ensemble to calculate its own uncertainty, a 16 member ensemble of direct reconstructions is created by reconstructing the observational input at different network states. Around the original stopping point of 550.000 iterations, the network state is extracted at 15 other iterations, each 500 training iterations apart. Thus, the resulting ensemble spread is not the same as previously with the indirect reconstructions but it does give a rough estimate of the uncertainty involved in the neural network reconstructions. A further discussion of these uncertainties is given in section \ref{uncertainty}.\\
	
	\begin{figure}[H]
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=1.0\linewidth]{plots/timeseries/Timeseries_obs_full}
		\caption{Estimates of monthly upper 700 m SPG OHC of one EnKF assimilation's ensemble member (red) and direct neural network reconstruction (green) during the pre-Argo and Argo-Era. The shaded areas represent the uncertainty caused by the corresponding ensemble spread.}
		\label{fig:timeseriesfullobs}
	\end{figure}
		
	The direct reconstruction OHC estimate shows higher disagreements to the assimilations (\ref{fig:timeseriesfullobs}), but still shows high correlation with an anomaly correlation of 0.89. Two time periods stand out in particular in this figure. At first, the direct reconstruction shows clear disagreement with the assimilation during the first 10 years from 1958 -- 1968. This behavior can again be related to the uncertainty of the assimilation with very few observational input already discussed in \ref{analysis_preargo}. \\


	\begin{figure}[H]
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=1.0\linewidth]{plots/timeseries/Timeseries_masked_full}
		\caption{Estimates of upper 700m SPG OHC at the grid points of observations. Displayed are the EnKF assimilations (blue), the observations (green), the direct reconstructions of observations (red) and the indirect reconstructions of masked assimilations (orange).}
		\label{fig:timeseriesmaskedfull}
	\end{figure}
	
	Additionally, especially during the peak around the year 2007, the direct reconstruction shows a higher deviation from the assimilation timeseries compared to the indirect reconstruction. In general, the direct reconstructions show a cool bias compared to the assimilation during most of the Argo-Era. To examine the reason for this bias and to put the direct reconstruction into the context of the EN4 observations, it is useful to compare the assimilation and the observations at the points of observations. \\	
	The OHC timeseries at the points of observations depicted in figure \ref{fig:timeseriesmaskedfull} shows that the observations possess a significant cool bias compared to the assimilation. The neural network reproduces its corresponding input very closely and thus it is not surprising that the bias from its input is then transferred to its overall estimation. However, the bias of the neural networks input has a smaller effect on its overall output during the pre-Argo-Era due to he smaller percentage of input points compared to the output. Furthermore, the bias between assimilation and observations is less distinct during the pre-Argo-Era. This bias is not unusual. Most assimilations show small biases towards the observations, which are caused by the model dynamics in the background. As the assimilation regards model and observations as uncertain it does not just integrate the exact observations value but finds a compromise between both factors (Brune, pers. comm.). In order to better interpret this bias it has to be put into the context of other OHC estimates.  \\
	
	\begin{figure}[H]
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=1.0\linewidth]{plots/timeseries/Timeseries_obs_full_annual}
		\caption{Estimates of annual running means of upper 700 m SPG OHC of one EnKF assimilation's ensemble member (red) and direct neural network reconstruction (grey) during the pre-Argo and Argo-Era. The shaded areas represent the uncertainty caused by the corresponding ensemble spread. In addition, the estimates of the IAP (orange) and EN4 (purple) are provided.}
		\label{fig:timeseriesobsfullannual}
	\end{figure}
	
	The IAP and EN4 OHC estimates shown in figure \ref{fig:timeseriesobsfullannual} agree notably well with each other while showing a significant cool bias compared to the neural network reconstruction and the EnKF assimilation in particular. During the Argo-era, the EN4 OHC estimate even shows a higher correlation to the direct reconstruction (0.98) than the assimilation (0.96). The difference during the pre-Argo-Era however is especially large in the first 10 years, where both timeseries show opposite anomalies. This difference indicates another uncertainty in the assimilation. While the low observational density can explain some of the differences, it is not enough to explain the entire discrepancy between the assimilation and the other OHC estimates. Koul (pers. comm.) has examined the EnKF assimilation closer and has concluded that the assimilation does not deliver reliable results directly after its start. Instead, Koul (pers. comm.) has shown that the assimilation requires time to adjust to the observations after its initial start. Therefore, it has been shown to possess high uncertainties until the start of the 1970s. This adjustment period fits well to the differences to the IAP and EN4 estimates and relaxes towards a simple positive bias with the start of the 1970s. The neural network reconstruction also shows much higher disagreement to the assimilation during that period, while it also clearly does not follow the other estimates. The still existing agreement between the neural network reconstruction and the assimilation indicates that the patterns responsible for this adjustment period are also somehow existent during the Argo-Era. If they were completely independent from the remaining assimilation, a neural network trained during the Argo-Era would then show higher differences during this unrelated adjustment period. \\
	
		
	\begin{figure}[H]
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=1.0\linewidth]{plots/timeseries/patcor_obs_full_annual}
		\caption{Pattern correlation of the annual running means of the EnKF assimilation and the direct neural network reconstruction (grey) and indirect neural network reconstruction (red).}
		\label{fig:patcorobsfullannual}
	\end{figure}
	
	
	During the remaining pre-Argo-Era the reconstruction then follows the assimilation quite closely, showing the same warm bias in comparison to the IAP and EN4 estimates. From the middle of the 1990s until the 2010s the neural network estimates a middle way between the EN4 and the EnKF estimates. This development of the reconstruction during the Argo-Era can be read as an indicator that the warm bias in the assimilation is in fact a result of a bias within the assimilation at the points of observations. If instead it was due to a different assessment of the physical processes, the neural network trained during that period would probably agree closer with the assimilation than it does with the EN4 analysis. \\		
	When visualizing the pattern correlation between observations reconstruction and the EnKF assimilations, it becomes obvious that there are more differences between both OHC estimates than a pure bias. Figure \ref{fig:patcorobsfullannual} depicts much lower correlations in the direct compared to the indirect reconstructions with a mean pattern correlation of 0.44. The comparison to the pattern correlation of the indirect reconstruction also shows that both show very similar behavior. Therefore, it can be assumed that during the times with low pattern correlation, the observations cause the assimilation to form spatial patterns that the neural network has not learned. The difference between observations and assimilation then only induces a bias into the pattern correlation. This bias also proves that the neural network integrates the patterns from the observations instead of just translating the value bias and then forcing the observations into the same spatial patterns as the assimilation. \\
	
	\begin{figure}[h]
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=1.0\linewidth]{plots/images/hc_200801_full_obs}
		\caption{Map of the EN4 observations (left), the EnKF assimilation (middle) and neural network direct reconstruction estimates of NA upper 700m anomaly OHC estimate in January 2008.}
		\label{fig:hc200801fullobs}
	\end{figure}
	
	
	Figure \ref{fig:hc200801fullobs} supports this hypothesis. It depicts the neural network's direct reconstruction of observations in 2018. However, the observations show clear regional differences compared to the assimilation. Even though the overall OHC estimates of reconstruction and assimilation agree very closely at that time, the regional patterns of the observations are incorporated more by the neural network. But, the image of the direct OHC reconstruction also shows that the neural network produces less pronounced spatial patterns on smaller scales. Beyond larger structures, such as the SPG cooling and the warming of the STG it shows very few smooth small scale patterns. So even though the neural network seems to replicate large scale observational patterns, it has difficulties in the resolution of smaller scale structures. This difficulty to produce smooth regional patterns is most likely also the reason for the low pattern correlation of the direct reconstruction during most of the pre-Argo-Era. Even though the overall OHC estimates agree during most of the 1970s and 1980s, the spatial patterns do not show a lot of resemblance on annual timescales. \\
	
	
	\section{Future: Reconstructing New Timesteps} \label{analysis_future}
	
	In a final analysis step, the utility of the neural network in the reconstruction of data from more recent time period is examined. The same trained neural network is given observations from October 2020 until October 2021. As the training data of the neural network only runs until October 2020, it has never see the observational patterns as well as data assimilations from that period. Therefore, this part is a test of the neural network's ability to replace the EnKF assimilations in its application as a reanalysis for present day timesteps. \\

Figure \ref{fig:misfitobsanhang} shows that the differences between both OHC estimates remain very low even after the end of the training period.
	
	
	\section{Neural Network Uncertainty} \label{uncertainty}
	
	\begin{figure}[h]
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=1.0\linewidth]{plots/uncertainty}
		\caption{Estimates of machine learning uncertainty surrounding the reconstruction of SPG OHC (blue line) and compared to the assimilation (red line). Shown are the uncertainty stemming from the reconstruction of the 16 assimilation members (darkest blue shade), the uncertainty stemming from 16 different stopping points of the training process (lighter blue shade) and the uncertainty stemming from the 5 completely new training instances of the neural network (lightest blue).}
		\label{fig:uncertainty}
	\end{figure}
	
	The analyses of the Argo and pre-Argo-Era reconstructions of the neural network have already described several sources of uncertainty in the use of neural networks. The first and most obvious source of uncertainty consists of the amount of points of observations. The neural network does come much closer to reproducing the assimilation during the pre-Argo-Era if the assimilation is masked with Argo-Era masks (figure \ref{fig:timeseries_assi_full_maskargo}). As discussed in section \ref{analysis_preargo}, this difference cannot be interpreted as a fault of the neural network but rather as a measure of uncertainty within the neural network's training data during times with low observational density. However, other sources of uncertainty, which stem directly from the neural network setup, have not been discussed yet. \\
	The first source of uncertainty inherent to the neural network is the length of its training. Even though there is a validation error considered in the choice of stopping point, it is only a rough estimation of the most accurate version of the neural network. This point can only be evaluated every 5000 iterations, when the model state is saved during the training process. Evaluating the network more often during the training process prolongs the training significantly and would thus not be sensible under a efficiency standpoint. Nonetheless, the remaining uncertainty stemming from the final iteration can be estimated. The uncertainty range around the OHC timeseries of the neural network reconstruction of the validation assimilation ensemble member in figure \ref{fig:uncertainty} includes additional terms. A 16 member machine learning ensemble is created by saving the model at 16 different points around 550000 iterations with each member 500 iterations apart from the next. The standard deviation of the resulting reconstructions is then shown as the second part of the reconstruction's uncertainty range. As previously, the first part depicts the ensemble spread of the 16 reconstructed assimilation members.\\
	The neural network's second source of uncertainty is the training process itself. The utilized training data is randomly sampled at each training iteration in order to prevent any biases stemming from its selection. Due to this random sampling the neural network develops slightly different at each training iteration. Therefore, the network could move into different local loss minima and consequentially into different network states. In order to estimate this uncertainty, the training process has been repeated five times. The resulting standard deviation of the five reconstructions of this ensemble member is depicted as the third part of the uncertainty range shown in figure \ref{fig:uncertainty}.
	
	\section{Time and Computational Resources} \label{resources}
	
	One of the main reasons to utilize machine learning in the context of data assimilation are the differences in computational resources used. In this section, the actual time and computational resources of the utilized partial convolutional U-net are evaluated and put into the context of the EnKF assimilation. The computational resources needed for all processes are measured in node hours at the high performance computing (HPC) system \textit{Levante} run by the German Climate Computing Center (DKRZ). The neural network is run on NVIDIA's A100 gpu nodes, while the assimilation's node hours are assumed to run on AMD EPYC CPUs (Milan). Further information on the Levante HPC system as well as detailed documentation of its technical setup can be viewed under \url{https://docs.dkrz.de}.\\
	The computational resources needed by both approaches have to be put into the context of different applications. Table \ref{table:resources} summarizes the computation times required by the neural network and the EnKF assimilation for different tasks or scenarios. The computational requirements are once estimated in node hours (NH) to approximate the overall computational costs. Additionally, the overall computation time (OCT) is estimated in hours, which allows for the running of parallel processes and thus approximates the time between start of all computations and the availability of the results. This estimate of the OCT assumes optimal node availability, which in the case of the neural network means 20 gpu nodes, while the assimilation requires 128 cpu nodes to run simultaneously. All resources required by the EnKF assimilations are estimated by \cite{brune_personal_2022}. The estimates given for the machine learning approach do not include the generation of training data. Thus, it has to be kept in mind that the neural network is dependent on the availability of assimilation or other similar model data in order to perform any reconstruction task. All estimates are rough approximations aiming at showcasing only the dimension of the time required. \\

	One of the largest differences in computational requirements between both approaches is the adjustment of the reconstruction with changes in observational data. These changes could include new bias corrections for measurements or the inclusion additional and elimination of existing observations. In the case of the machine learning, the trained network would just be required to reconstruct the adjusted observational data. Training the neural network again would be unnecessary, because small differences between training and testing datasets are to expected for example due to assimilation biases. Thus, an adjustment in observations would not significantly impact the performance of an already trained network. Therefore, it would perform such an adjustment very efficiently. The assimilation however would be required to generate an entirely new ensemble, which leads to significantly larger computational requirements. Another aspect in which both approaches differ significantly is the generation of new ensemble members. Due to the fact that EnKF assimilations work only as an ensemble approach, the generation of one new ensemble member requires the recalculation of the entire ensemble. \\
