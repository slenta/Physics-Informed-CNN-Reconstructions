	\chapter{Results} \label{results}
	
	At first, the U-net is trained with assimilations as well as masks only from the Argo era. Thus, it is set up as well as possible to reproduce argo era assimilations. The training process is then conducted until the loss parameters do not show any significant decrease anymore. Manual selection has shown that the most important loss parameters for the reconstruction of masked temperature profiles are those monitoring the direct misfit of each gridpoint and the total variation loss between neighboring gridpoints. Thus, their multiplicators have been upgraded in this version of the convolutional U-net and their development will be shown to indicate the state of training of the neural network. \\
	
	Figure \ref{fig:loss} shows the development of the hole and valid loss functions of the network training on reconstructing the first depth layer, the sea surface temperatures (SSTs). After around 1000000 training iterations, the network's loss function is not reduced significantly anymore with additional training iterations. It has reached a stable plateau. Thus, the first part of the training process is stopped and followed by 200000 iterations of finetuning. In the process of finetuning a lower learning rate of $5 \cd 10^{-5}$ ensures that the network moves as close as possible towards the local minimum it would be circling around with a higher learning rate. Figure \ref{fig:loss_val} depicts the development of the validation loss. Validation is conducted by giving the network several timesteps of one of the held back assimilation ensemble members (r15i1p1f1) to reconstruct. The resulting validation error is then calculated parallel to the training error. The progression of the validation loss shows that the network has not adjusted too much to the training data up to 1.000.000 training iterations. Thus, overfitting of the network is prevented. \\
	
	
	\section{Present: Argo Era Reconstructions} \label{analysis_argo}
	
	After the training process is complete, the Argo era assimilation data of another held back ensemble member (r2i1p1f1) is masked with the corresponding observational patterns. These masks are then given to the trained neural network for reconstruction. The reconstructed temperature profiles are then converted to an OHC timeseries. While the network is trained with the masked assimilations from the entire NA, the data for all other analysis steps is limited to just the SPG. Thus, the neural network is given the opportunity to also learn the SPG surrounding spatial patterns, while the remaining analysis is focused on a region that is of higher interest to current research. Therefore, the results can also be better compared to other OHC estimates. Figure (\ref{fig:argo_timeseries_assi}) shows the timeseries of Argo era SPG OHC for both the EnKF assimilations (red) and the neural network reconstructions of masked assimilations (blue). The uncertainty range around both timeseries represents the corresponding ensemble spread. In the case of the neural network, this ensemble is produced by reconstructing each assimilation ensemble member individually. \\

	\subsection{Depth Training} \label{depth_training}
	
	These first results were produced by training 20 separate neural networks, one for each depth step, and combining the results after training to calculate the overall OHC. This process does not allow for cross connections between the layers. Alternatively, the different depth steps can also be inserted into adjoining input channels of one neural network, thus also allowing for the neural network to from vertical connections in the temperature profile. This approach will from here on out be called \textit{depth training}. The additional connections incorporated in \textit{depth training} also introduce a multitude of new degrees of freedom. Thus, the neural network requires a lot more time to train as well as higher computational resources. Nonetheless, this approach of training a neural network to reconstruct the entire temperature profiles should be considered, if it yields higher correlation to the assimilation in time as well as in space. Figure \ref{fig:argo_timeseries_assi_depth} shows the OHC timeseries produced by this new reconstruction, again compared to the EnKF assimilation. \\
	
	
	The comparison shows that the depth training produces an OHC estimate with significantly lower correlation to the assimilation. Similarly, the pattern correlation between both estimates is lower for the entire timespan (\ref{fig:patterncorr_assi_argo_depth}). Thus, it can be assumed that this depth training does not present an equally valid alternative to the single layer training. Furthermore, as all single layer neural network can be trained simultaneous, it is faster by around a factor 20 compared to the depth training for temperature profiles with 20 depth steps. Therefore, the previous single layer training approach will be utilized for the remaining analysis. \\
	To determine the exact causes for the higher uncertainty in this reconstruction would require additional testing. Most likely, the neural network is overburdened with the free input of additional degrees of freedom due to the vertical connections. It is often assumed that additional information results in better neural network performance. However, increasing the dimensionality of the training data while keeping its amount the same gives the neural network less possibility to train every single dimension \cite{bach_breaking_2017}. Thus, it has difficulty reconstructing the single temperature layers without additional training data, which then in turn degrades the overall performance. 
	
	
	\section{Past: Extension to pre-Argo Era} \label{analysis_preargo}
	
	
	The next step to evaluating, if the neural network has the potential to replace EnKF data assimilation, consists of extending the time window of reconstruction to the pre-Argo Era. In order to prepare the neural network for the reconstruction of the observational patterns of the pre-Argo era, the training process has to include pre-Argo masks. Therefore, the training data, which still only stems from the Argo era, is masked with observational patterns from the entire period from 1958--2020. Otherwise, the neural network's parameters are kept constant and trained with single layer depth training. Again, the training is stopped after 1.000.000 iterations of normal training and 200.000 additional iterations of finetuning. The training curves of the relevant hole and valid loss are shown in figure \ref{fig:error_preargo}.\\

	Figure \ref{fig:full_timeseries_assi_annual} depicts these annual running means. Supported by the depiction of the misfit between both timeseries in figure \ref{fig:misfit_full_assi}, it becomes obvious that the difference between both timeseries consists of more than just increased variability due to higher uncertainty caused by less observations. Instead, some trends become obvious in this annual timeseries. Firstly, the assimilation shows much higher interannual variability during the first ten years of the timeseries. Secondly, the neural network shows a consistent warm bias during the following ten years. The two main options as causes for these differences are on the one hand an overfitting of the neural network to the training period. It has to be considered a possibility that the neural network does not recognize assimilation patterns from the beginning of the pre-Argo era, if they resemble those of the training period too little. On the other hand, it is also possible that the differences are caused just by the difference in observational density between the Argo and pre-Argo era. Then, these differences could be utilized as indicators of uncertainties in the assimilation beyond its ensemble spread. 
	
	\begin{figure}
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=1.0\linewidth]{plots/timeseries/Timeseries_full_assi_maskargo}
		\caption{Estimates of monthly upper 700 m North Atlantic OHC of one EnKF assimilation's ensemble member (red) and its neural network reconstruction (blue) during the pre-Argo and Argo era. For the reconstruction, the assimilation member has only been masked with Argo era observational patterns. The shaded areas represent the uncertainty caused by the corresponding ensemble spread.}
		\label{fig:timeseriesfullassimaskargo}
	\end{figure}
	
	
	Figure \ref{fig:full_timeseries_assi_argomask} supports this second hypothesis. It shows reconstructions over the same period, with the difference that the observational patterns of the Argo era are also used during the pre-Argo era to mask the assimilations. Therefore, this reconstruction gives an estimate on how much of the reconstruction uncertainty comes from the amount of observations. However, it also prevents the neural network from implementing assimilation patterns from the Argo era in the reconstruction of the pre-Argo assimilations. By using the Argo era observational patterns, the high observational density pushes the neural network very close to the assimilation without leaving it its own room for interpretation. As it is assumed that the assimilation is more accurate over the training period, the difference between reconstruction and assimilation thus cannot be interpreted as a fault of the neural network. Instead, it rather shows the uncertainty the assimilation incorporates, if the underlying model cannot be supported and nudged by sufficient observations. \\
	Closer examination of the reconstructions and assimilation during the first decade of the timeseries can help explain the observed uncertainty in that time period. In figure \ref{fig:full_timeseries_assi} the assimilation shows much stronger interannual variations in SPG OHC at the start of the 1960s than the neural network reconstructions. \cite{vimal_personal} has already examined similar EnKF assimilations during that period and found that the assimilation seems to possess much higher uncertainty during that time than its ensemble spread suggests. The biggest reason for this uncertainty is the dependence of the assimilation on observational input to correct for biases in the model, for example due to its positioning of the North Atlantic current. But also other uncertainties in the model get highlighted when there are no observations available to assist the assimilation in its estimate.	
	The differences between both timeseries support that assumption. It shows that the assimilation's behavior during this period is not consistent to the spatial patterns and geophysical processes during the neural network's training period.\\
	
	
	Figure \ref{fig:1965_assi} visualizes the OHC of both the assimilation and reconstruction during that time period in the form of a 10 year mean. Both versions differ most notably in the central NA, where the assimilation shows strong negative anomalies, while the neural network reconstruction depicts close to no anomalies in that region. Similar differences have already been described in other publications and can be related to what is known as the North Atlantic current's \textit{North-West corner}. Many climate models, such as the MPI-ESM underlying this EnKF assimilation, are known to represent the gulf stream in the central NA further south than its observed flow. Therefore, the assimilation shows strong negative anomalies in the region of this North-West corner whenever there are no observations available to nudge the underlying model towards a correct representation of the gulf stream's flow. As the neural network is only trained in a period with high observational density in the entire NA, it only learns a correct representation of the gulf streams flow. Correspondingly, the reconstructions do not show the same negative anomalies in this region, when there are no observations available to suggest anything different.\\
	
	Thus, it can also be assumed that parts of the differences between assimilation and neural network reconstruction during the early 1960s can be attributed to a false representation of the North-West corner, while from the 1970s onwards at least sporadic observations are available in this region causing a different assimilation output. Figure \ref{fig:corr_start_assi} supports this analysis, as it shows that the gridwise correlation between reconstruction and assimilation is especially low in the region of the North-West corner during the first 10 years, while it depicts higher correlation in the rest of the pre-Argo era with the steady rise of observations. This whole representation of the North-West and its corresponding effects on the SPG OHC timeseries suggests that a neural network trained with assimilation data during a time with high observational density can be able to detect model uncertainties in specific regions. Additionally, it can provide an estimate of the overall uncertainty not contained within the assimilation's ensemble spread. \\
	During the remainder of the 20th century reconstruction and assimilation mostly agree within their respective uncertainty ranges. Nonetheless, the reconstruction shows a positive bias until the beginning of the 1980s. This positive bias could still be a remainder of the North-West corner depiction and vanishes slowly but steadily in the 1970s and 1980s. Figure \ref{fig:assi_1970s} shows that during the first half of the 1970s the assimilation still shows negative anomalies characteristic to the North-West corner. Afterwards, both timeseries agree with each other until and throughout the argo era. \\
	
	
	Nontheless, figure \ref{fig:assi_1990_full} shows that during the late pre-Argo, the neural network does not capture spatial patterns as well as previously shown in section \ref{results_argo}.	Given the still existing scarcity of observations during this late pre-Argo era, theses differences are not surprising. However, the large scale structures agree with the EnKF assimilation.\\
	
	
	During the Argo era, the neural network reconstruction also shows less agreement with the assimilation OHC estimate. Figure \ref{fig:assi_2019_full} depicts a comparison between the OHC reconstruction of the neural network trained with all masks and the one trained only with Argo era masks for the year 2019. This comparison shows that even though the network trained with all masks has also been trained with Argo era masks, it has greater difficulties reproducing the exact smaller scale structures of the assimilation. The differences between both neural network reconstructions show that there is a tradeoff between reproducing the pre-Argo and Argo era assimilations. The inclusion of pre-Argo masks creates more freedom for the network to change the model reality, while giving only high observational density masks into the training pushes the neural networks reconstructions very close towards the assimilation. However, it has to be put into the context that on larger scales the neural network trained with all masks still reproduces the assimilation within its uncertainty for every month in the Argo era. \\
	
	\subsection{Direct Observations Reconstruction} \label{analysis_direct}
	
	The reconstruction of masked assimilation aims mainly at assessing the neural network's ability to reproduce EnKF assimilations and identifying uncertainties in the assimilation stemming from the underlying model. Another uncertainty that cannot be examined with this approach is the assimilation's integration of observations. Additionally, it is not possible to estimate the neural network's potential to replace data assimilation, when the neural network is never given actual observations as input. Thus, the neural network is now given the EN4 observations from 1958 until 2021 as input in a next evaluation step. To clarify the terminology, the reconstruction of observations by the neural network will from now on be called \textit{direct reconstructions}, while the reconstruction of masked assimilations will be referred to as \textit{indirect reconstructions}.\\

In general, the direct reconstructions show a cool bias compared to the assimilation during the Argo era. However, the bias of the neural networks input has a smaller effect on its overall output during the pre-Argo era due to he smaller percentage of input points compared to the output. Furthermore, the bias between assimilation and observations is less distinct during the pre-Argo era. This bias is not unusual. Most assimilations show small biases towards the observations, which are caused by the model dynamics in the background. As the assimilation regards model and observations as uncertain it does not just integrate the exact observations value but finds a compromise between both factors \cite{brune_personal_2020}. In order to better interpret this bias it has to be put into the context of other OHC estimates.  \\

Beyond larger structures, such as the SPG cooling and the warming of the STG it shows very few smooth small scale patterns. So even though the neural network seems to replicate large scale observational patterns, it has difficulties in the resolution of smaller scale structures.

	\section{Future: Reconstructing New Timesteps} \label{analysis_future}
	
	In a final analysis step, it will now be examined, in how far the neural network can also be used to reconstruct data from more recent time periods. The same trained neural network will now be given observations from October 2020 until October 2021. As the training data of the neural network only runs until October 2020, it has never see the observational patterns as well as data assimilations from that period. Therefore, this part is a test of the neural network's ability to replace the EnKF assimilations in its application as a reanalysis for present day timesteps. 
	
	
	Figure \ref{fig:timeseries_anhang_obs} depicts the resulting reconstruction. The start of the new observation input in October 2020 is marked by the red line. It can be seen that each single monthly OHC value lies within the uncertainty of the assimilation's ensemble spread. The neural network's estimate even lies closer to the assimilation than just one standard deviation would suggest. 
	
	
	The misfit between both timeseries supports that assumption. Figure \ref{fig:misfit_anhang_obs} shows that the differences between both OHC estimates go down, even though the reconstruction period lies outside of the training period. The pattern correlation from the period shown in figure \ref{fig:patterncorr_anhang_obs} agrees with that impression.
	
	\section{Neural Network Uncertainty} \label{uncertainty}
	
	The analyses of the Argo and pre-Argo era reconstructions of the neural network have already described several sources of uncertainty in the use of neural networks. The first and most obvious source of uncertainty consists of the amount of points of observations. The neural network does come much closer to reproducing the assimilation during the pre-Argo era if the assimilation is masked with Argo era masks (figure \ref{fig:timeseriesfullassimaskargo}). As discussed in section \ref{analysis_preargo}, this difference cannot be interpreted as a fault of the neural network but rather as a measure of uncertainty within the neural network's training data during times with low observational density. However, other sources of uncertainty, which stem directly from the neural network setup, have not been discussed yet. \\
	The first source of uncertainty inherent to the neural network is the length of its training. Even though there is a validation error considered in the choice of stopping point, it is only a rough estimation of the most accurate version of the neural network. This point can only be evaluated every 5000 iterations, when the model state is saved during the training process. Evaluating the network more often during the training process prolongs the training significantly and would thus not be sensible under a efficiency standpoint. Nonetheless, the remaining uncertainty stemming from the final iteration can be estimated. Figure \ref{fig:uncertainty} shows the neural network reconstruction of Assimilation ensemble member r2i1p1f1 again after 575000 training iterations. However, the uncertainty range around the OHC timeseries includes additional terms. A 16 member machine learning ensemble is created by saving the model at 16 different points around 575000 iterations with each member 500 iterations apart from the next. The standard deviation of the resulting reconstructions is then shown as the second part of the reconstruction's uncertainty range. As previously, the first part depicts the ensemble spread of the 16 reconstructed assimilation members.\\
	The neural network's second source of uncertainty is the training process itself. The utilized training data is randomly sampled at each training iteration in order to prevent any biases stemming from its selection. Due to this random sampling the neural network develops slightly different at each training iteration. Therefore, the network could move into different local loss minima and consequentially into different network states. In order to estimate this uncertainty, the training process has been repeated five times. The resulting standard deviation of the five reconstruction of this ensemble member is depicted as the third fracture of the uncertainty range shown in figure \ref{fig:uncertainty}.
	
	\section{Time and Computational Resources}
	
	One of the main reasons to utilize machine learning in the context of data assimilation are the differences in computational resources used. In this section, the actual time and computational resources of the utilized partial convolutional U-net are evaluated and put into the context of the EnKF assimilation. The computational resources needed for all processes are measured in node hours at the high performance computing (HPC) system \textit{Levante} run by the \textit{Deutsche Klimarechenzentrum} (DKRZ). The neural network is run on NVIDIA's A100 gpu nodes, while the assimilation's node hours are assumed to run on AMD EPYC CPUs (Milan). Further information on the Levante HPC system as well as detailed documentation of its technical setup can be viewed under \url{https://docs.dkrz.de}.\\
	The computational resources needed by both approaches have to be put into the context of different applications. Table \ref{table:resources} summarizes the computation times required by the neural network and the EnKF assimilation for different tasks or scenarios. The computational requirements are once estimated in node hours (NH) to approximate the overall computational costs. Additionally, the overall computation time (OCT) is estimated in hours, which allows for the running of parallel processes and thus approximates the time between start of all computations and the availability of the results. This estimate of the OCT assumes optimal node availability, which in the case of the neural network means 20 gpu nodes, while the assimilation requires 128 cpu nodes to run simultaneously. All resources required by the EnKF assimilations are estimated by \cite{brune_personal_2022}. The estimates given for the machine learning approach do not include the generation of training data. Thus, it has to be kept in mind that the neural network is dependent on the availability of assimilation or other similar model data in order to perform any reconstruction task. All estimates are rough approximations aiming at showcasing only the dimension of the time required. \\

	One of the largest differences in computational requirements between both approaches is the adjustment of the reconstruction with changes in observational data. These changes could include new bias corrections for measurements or the inclusion additional and elimination of existing observations. In the case of the machine learning, the trained network would just be required to reconstruct the adjusted observational data. Training the neural network again would be unnecessary, because small differences between training and testing datasets are to expected for example due to assimilation biases. Thus, an adjustment in observations would not significantly impact the performance of an already trained network. Therefore, it would preform such an adjustment very efficiently. The assimilation however would be required to generate an entirely new ensemble, which leads to significantly larger computational requirements. Another aspect in which both approaches differ significantly is the generation of new ensemble members. Due to the fact that EnKF assimilations work only as an ensemble approach, the generation of one new ensemble member requires the recalculation of the entire ensemble. \\
