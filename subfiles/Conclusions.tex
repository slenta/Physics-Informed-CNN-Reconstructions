	\chapter{Summary and Conclusions} \label{conclusions}
	
	This study aims at examining if neural networks, more specifically partial convolutional U-nets, are able to reproduce or replace EnKF data assimilations of SPG OHC. While training with Argo-Era masks on Argo-Era EnKF assimilations, the partial convolutional U-net is able to reconstruct masked assimilations from the Argo-Era well within the assimilation's ensemble spread with a correlation of 0.99. The timeseries of SPG OHC produced by the neural network reproduces even the monthly variability of the reconstructed assimilation member. Furthermore, the neural network is able to reconstruct regional OHC patterns and even small scale structures. This assessment is supported by a mean pattern correlation of 0.87 over the entire Argo-Era. \\
	When these results are extended to the pre-Argo-Era, the agreement between assimilation and neural network reconstruction sinks slightly with a timeseries anomaly correlation of 0.93 over the entire time span. Both disagree in particular during the first 10 years. These differences however can be explained by a mixture of the low observational density and the assimilation's adjustment phase (Koul, pers. comm.). After the first ten years the agreement increases to mostly within one to two standard deviations. The neural network still shows a warm bias compared to the assimilation. The pattern correlation between both OHC estimates is also lower during the pre-Argo-Era compared to the Argo-Era. Nonetheless, with a mean pattern correlation of 0.62 before the onset of the Argo project, the spatial patterns of the neural network reconstructions continue to show significant correlation to the assimilation.  \\
	The neural network's direct reconstruction of observations shows larger differences to the assimilation, but still possessing an anomaly correlation of 0.92. These differences can mostly be explained by the bias inherent to the assimilation at the points of observation. The pattern correlation between assimilation and direct reconstruction is significantly lower with a mean of 0.42 than that of the indirect reconstruction. The differences in pattern correlation as well as overall OHC are especially strong during the SPG OHC peak around 2005. However, both EN4 and IAP estimate show a cool bias towards the assimilation during the Argo-Era similar to that of the neural network reconstruction.\\
	Finally, an analysis of the computational resources used by both the EnKF assimilations and the neural network shows that the most significant differences occur, when the neural network's ability to reconstruct independent of another training instance is utilized. The generation of another ensemble member or the reconstruction of adjusted observational data show differences in node hours of up to a factor 1000. \\
	
	Given the close agreement between the neural network reconstruction and the EnKF assimilation it can be concluded that partial convolutional U-nets are able to reproduce EnKF assimilations in the Argo as well as the pre-Argo-Era. Especially the comparison to other OHC estimates, such as the shown IAP and EN4 analyses, shows that the neural network can reproduce its training input much closer than its uncertainty in the comparison of different mapping methods.\\
	Additionally, the results show that the neural network reconstructions are also able to replace the EnKF assimilation in their function as a OHC reanalysis for new timesteps. Even monthly variations in timesteps outside of its training data are reproduced by the neural network when directly reconstructing observations from the Argo-Era onwards. \\
	Furthermore, this study has also shown that neural network reconstructions can take a role in estimating the uncertainty of assimilations outside of the their ensemble spread. The neural network is able to transfer spatial patterns from its well observed training period to earlier time spans. Thus, it can be concluded that partial convolutional U-nets can also be utilized to detect uncertainties and inconsistencies in OHC assimilations. The results of the direct reconstruction of observations highlight inconsistencies between assimilation and observations at the observed grid points. Therefore, partial convolutional U-nets can also help estimate the uncertainty stemming from biases and differences between assimilation and observations at the observed gridpoints.\\
	Finally, the comparison of computational requirements between both methods show that neural network have the potential to significantly speed up the assimilation of individual variables. Especially the flexible buildup of neural networks can speed up specific functions related to data assimilation, such as the generation of new ensemble members. With growing resolution and larger datasets and ensembles, this difference between the computational requirements is bound to grow even further (Brune, pers. comm.). \\
	In general, this study has shown that neural networks have the potential to significantly impact data assimilation of ocean variables in two ways. On the one hand, neural networks have the ability to reproduce assimilations and other mapping methods. On the other hand, they are capable of giving insights into the uncertainty of other assimilation techniques, especially for undersampled regions and variables. Ultimately, this study is only one of the first steps towards machine learning greatly impacting data assimilation and the climate sciences in general.\\
